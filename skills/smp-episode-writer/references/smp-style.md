# Scripted SMP Style Reference

## What Makes Scripted SMP Dialogue Work
(Derived from analysis of high-quality scripted SMP content)

### The Core Tension
Scripted SMP sits between two extremes:
- Too scripted = feels fake, characters sound like robots reading lines
- Too loose = feels like casual Let's Play, no narrative tension

The sweet spot: **natural energy with intentional structure.** Characters feel like real people, but every exchange moves the story.

### Dialogue Patterns That Work

**Interrupted sentences** — characters cut each other off, finish each other's thoughts, talk over each other in chaos:
```
WUMBO: Okay so the plan is—
EGG: He's right behind us.
WUMBO: I know, I'm trying to—
EGG: WUMBO.
WUMBO: I SEE HIM.
```

**Name-calling mid-sentence** — real players use each other's names constantly. Keep this:
```
EGG: Bro, I don't have a shield.
WUMBO: Egg, just run. Egg. EGG.
```

**Deadpan reactions to absurd situations** — the comedy often comes from underreaction:
```
WUMBO: So he basically kidnapped me and put me in a flying machine.
EGG: ...okay.
WUMBO: Just saying.
```

**Characters with different default registers**:
- The serious strategist who keeps redirecting chaos into plans
- The chaotic one who improvises and panics
- The neutral NPC/third-party who has their own agenda
- The comedic foil who doesn't understand gravity of the situation

**Subtext in negotiation scenes** — when two characters want opposite things:
```
DIZZY: Honestly, I couldn't care less why he wants you dead.
WUMBO: If you were going to kill me here, you'd have done it already.
[beat]
DIZZY: ...
WUMBO: I'm right, aren't I.
```

### Pacing Principles for SMP

**Scene length**: Most scenes 30 sec – 2 min. Cut the moment the scene's purpose is achieved.

**The "and then..." test**: If your scenes connect with "and then... and then... and then..." you have no escalation. Replace with "but... therefore..." — each scene creates a problem the next must solve.

**Breathing room**: After any big combat or revelation, insert a short 30-second scene of characters reacting, regrouping, making a quip. Then back into tension.

**Information reveals**: Don't give the audience everything at once. Each scene should answer one question and raise a new one.

### The UnstableSMP Model (What Made Their Recent Era Work)
- Strong character identities with consistent voices
- Every episode has one clear central mystery or goal
- Comedic beats earned through character contrast, not random jokes
- Each episode ends on a hook — either a cliffhanger or a shift in power
- Third-party characters (kings, merchants, tavern owners) have their own logic and agenda
- Combat is chaotic but has narrative stakes — winning or losing actually matters

### VO / Narrator Lines
Use VO sparingly. Best used for:
- Time skips: "Three hours later, we were no closer to an answer."
- Context for the audience: "The last time I'd seen this player was..."
- Retrospective irony: Set something up that the audience knows will go wrong

VO should sound like a person telling a story to a friend, not a documentary narrator.

### Arabic/Egyptian SMP Specific Notes
- Code-switching between formal and casual is natural and adds character
- Exclamations (يلا / والله / بجد) can be used in stage directions for tone
- Character names and server locations should feel grounded in the world's lore
- Humor often comes from cultural understatement and deadpan delivery

### Scene Templates

**The Plan Scene** (characters decide what to do next):
- Open with disagreement on approach
- One character proposes something risky/unorthodox
- Other character resists, then reluctantly agrees
- End on commitment — they go do the thing
- NEVER let them just agree immediately — that's boring

**The Confrontation Scene** (protagonist faces antagonist):
- Antagonist has power advantage
- Protagonist uses words/wit as a weapon
- A secret is revealed (partial — not everything)
- Scene ends before resolution — leave audience wanting more

**The Failure Scene** (things go wrong):
- Start with the plan working
- Introduce one variable that breaks it
- Characters scramble
- Ends in retreat or loss — but with new information gained

**The Alliance Scene** (characters join forces):
- Third party initially refuses
- Protagonist makes a case that appeals to the third party's SELF-INTEREST (not their morality)
- A condition is set
- Uneasy agreement — trust not fully established
