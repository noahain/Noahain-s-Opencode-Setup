# Screenwriting Craft Reference

## Core Principles

### Show, Don't Tell
Convey everything through action, image, and behavior — never exposition or internal monologue.
- BAD: "Noah is nervous."
- GOOD: "Noah fidgets with his inventory slots, keeps glancing at the horizon."

### Conflict is Everything
Every scene needs conflict — external (a fight, a chase) or internal (a moral dilemma, a choice).
Ask per scene: **What does this character want? What's in their way?**
If neither answer is clear, the scene shouldn't exist.

### Subtext
What a character doesn't say is as important as what they do.
- Two characters arguing about supplies are really arguing about trust.
- A character making a joke when they're scared.
- A character agreeing too quickly.

### Dialogue Rules
- Each character has a distinct voice. Read lines aloud — you should know who's speaking without seeing the name.
- Cut the first and last line of any dialogue exchange. The real scene usually starts and ends in the middle.
- Avoid "on the nose" dialogue — characters who say exactly what they feel. Real people are indirect.
- Less is more. Shorter lines hit harder.

### The Three-Act Structure (compressed for SMP)
- **Act 1**: Set up the world, the character, the problem. End with a commitment.
- **Act 2A**: The protagonist tries their plan. It partially works but complications arise. Midpoint shifts everything.
- **Act 2B**: Things fall apart. The plan fails. The protagonist hits bottom.
- **Act 3**: The protagonist tries again with new understanding. Climax. Resolution.

### Scene Structure (Micro)
Every scene is a tiny three-act:
1. Character enters scene wanting something.
2. They meet resistance.
3. They leave having gained, lost, or changed something — even slightly.

Scenes where nothing changes are cut.

### Theme
Every episode should have a central dramatic argument — a truth being explored.
- "You can't solve everything alone."
- "Trust has to be earned, not assumed."
- "Running from danger never works."

Weave it into dialogue naturally. One character states the anti-theme early. The protagonist proves or disproves it by the end.

### Pacing
- **Rhythm**: Alternate intense scenes with quieter character moments.
- **Tempo**: Short scenes = fast pace. Long scenes = slow, deliberate tension.
- **Breathing room**: After a major action beat, give the audience (and characters) 30 seconds to process.

### The Treatment Workflow (for pre-writing)
1. Logline — one sentence
2. Character summaries — who's in this and what they want
3. Synopsis — 3–5 sentences on the full arc
4. Act summary — one paragraph per act
5. Beat sheet — 10–15 key moments
6. Scene list — 20–40 scenes with location and purpose
7. Write

Use this flow when developing an episode from scratch. Don't write dialogue until the structure is solid.

### The Opening Image / Final Image Rule
The opening shot and closing shot should be mirror images — same location, angle, or motif — but showing that something has changed. This is the visual proof of the episode's theme.

### The Rewrite Mindset
First draft = get the story out. Rewrite = make it good.
On rewrite, ask per scene:
- Does this scene advance plot OR reveal character? (It must do at least one.)
- Is this the most interesting version of this scene?
- Can this be shorter?
